Precipitation: What are they and how are they formed?

Precipitation refers to any form of water particles that fall to the Earth's surface from the atmosphere. It includes:

  • Rain
  • Snow
  • Sleet
  • Hail
  • Drizzle
  • ice crystals

We'll be going over them shortly.

Rain

Rain is all about water constantly moving around our planet, in a giant cycle called the water cycle https://www.nationalgeographic.org/encyclopedia/water-cycle/.

1. Sun's heat: The sun heats up the water in oceans, lakes, and even puddles. This warmth causes the water to evaporate, changing from a liquid to a gas called water vapor.

2. Up, up, and away: Water vapor is lighter than air, so it rises high up into the atmosphere, where it's much cooler.

3. Condensation station: As the water vapor cools down, it condenses around tiny particles like dust, forming tiny water droplets. These droplets clump together to form clouds.

4. Growing droplets: Inside the clouds, the droplets bump into each other and merge, getting bigger and heavier.

5. Gravity wins: When the droplets become too heavy to float in the clouds, they fall down to Earth as rain!

Rain is crucial for life on Earth. It refills our freshwater supplies, like lakes and rivers, and nourishes plants to grow. The whole process is constantly happening, keeping our planet watered and balanced.

Snow

Snow is like magic crystals made from frozen water vapor high up in the sky.

1. Chilly atmosphere: The key ingredient for snow is cold temperatures, way below freezing (0째C or 32째F). With enough moisture in the air, water vapor skips the liquid stage altogether and transforms directly into ice crystals.

2. Ice crystal nuclei: These ice crystals form around microscopic dust particles in the air, acting like ice cube trays. As they float around in clouds, they attract more water vapor, which freezes onto the crystals.

3. Crystal design shop: Depending on air temperature and humidity, the ice crystals grow branches and flatten out, creating the beautiful snowflake shapes we know. Each snowflake is unique because of these varying conditions!

4. Gravity's pull: Once the ice crystals get heavy enough, they fall to Earth as snow!

Snow plays a vital role in our environment. It acts like a giant white blanket, insulating the ground and helping plants survive harsh winters. As it melts, it provides freshwater for rivers and ecosystems. So next time you see snow falling, remember it's a beautiful and crucial part of our planet's weather cycle.

Sleet

First, What is Sleet? it's an unfamiliar word for most of us. but don't worry! I got you!

Sleet is a type of winter precipitation distinct from snow, rain, and hail. It forms during a temperature inversion, where a warm layer of air exists above a colder layer near the ground.

1. Temperature Inversion: The key player is a temperature inversion. This occurs when a warm layer of air sits above a colder layer near the ground. Normally, temperature decreases as you move higher in the atmosphere.

2. Snow's Melty Journey: As snow falls from the clouds, it passes through the warm layer. This partial melting transforms the snowflakes into slushy drops.

3. Refreeze Rush: These slushy drops then plunge into the colder layer near the ground. The sudden drop in temperature rapidly freezes the slush, creating the characteristic ice pellets of sleet.

4. Bouncing on Arrival: Sleet pellets are typically smaller than peas and have a smooth, translucent appearance. Unlike snowflakes, they're denser and tend to bounce when they hit the ground.

5. Confusing Cousin: It's important to distinguish sleet from freezing rain. Freezing rain occurs when rain freezes upon contact with the cold surface, creating a glaze of ice. Sleet, on the other hand, freezes in the air before reaching the ground.

Sleet can accumulate on surfaces, making roads slippery and hazardous. It can also weigh down tree branches, leading to potential power outages. So, next time you encounter sleet, you'll understand the interesting atmospheric battle that created those icy little projectiles.

Hail

now this word seems actually familiar! let me explain more to you.

1. High-Rise Hustle: Hail forms high up in the intense, swirling world of thunderstorms. Powerful updrafts of air act like a conveyor belt, relentlessly carrying water droplets up into the frigid upper reaches of the cloud.

2. Supercool Science: At these high altitudes, temperatures are well below freezing (0째C or 32째F). However, under certain conditions, liquid water droplets can exist in a special state called supercooled liquid water. In this state, the water remains liquid even though the temperature is below freezing! Scientists believe tiny impurities or contaminants in the water droplet hinder the formation of ice crystals, preventing the water from freezing.

3. Ice Crystal Seeding: These supercooled droplets collide with tiny particles like dust or ice crystals already present in the cloud. This collision acts as a trigger, initiating the rapid freezing of the supercooled water onto the ice crystal. Think of it as finding a seed for crystal growth; the contact with the ice crystal provides a surface for the supercooled water to rapidly freeze and solidify.

4. Accretion Accumulation: The newly formed ice crystal then gets tossed around by the updraft, encountering more supercooled water droplets. As these droplets come into contact with the hailstone, they freeze instantly, adding to its mass. This process of accumulating ice layers continues as the hailstone travels through the updraft and encounters more supercooled water.

5. The Great Escape: As the hailstone grows heavier, the updraft eventually weakens. No longer strong enough to hold the hailstone aloft, gravity takes over, and the hailstone falls back to Earth.

6. The Journey Down: On its way down, the hailstone might be re-entrained by the updraft, bouncing back up into the cloud. This rise and fall allows for more ice layers to build up, creating the characteristic layered look of some hailstones.

7. Size Matters: The size of hail depends on the strength of the updrafts, the amount of supercooled water available, and the duration the hail spends growing within the cloud. Hailstones can range in size from tiny pellets to those as big as softballs!

Hail can be a destructive force, damaging crops, denting cars, and even injuring people and animals

Drizzle

Drizzle refers to precipitation characterized by miniscule liquid water droplets that fall at a slow and steady rate. These droplets typically measure less than 0.5 millimeters in diameter, resulting in a light and misty quality to the precipitation.Drizzle forms through a process of gradual condensation within clouds, primarily stratiform clouds and stratocumulus clouds.

1. Cloud Formation: The journey begins with the presence of moist air. As this air rises due to various atmospheric processes, it cools down. Colder air holds less moisture, causing the water vapor in the rising air to condense around tiny particles like dust or salt. This condensation leads to the formation of a cloud.

2. Slow and Steady Condensation: In the case of drizzle, the condensation process is relatively slow and continuous within stratiform clouds. These sheet-like clouds often have vast areas where air masses slowly rise and cool. Unlike rain-producing clouds, where condensation can be more vigorous, the slow, steady nature of condensation in stratiform clouds doesn't allow the water droplets to grow very large.

3. Limited Coalescence: Coalescence, the merging of smaller droplets into larger ones, plays a crucial role in rain formation. However, drizzle formation often experiences limited coalescence due to the small size of the droplets and the weaker updrafts within stratiform clouds. The updrafts might not be strong enough to keep the droplets colliding frequently, hindering their growth into rain-sized drops.

4. Reaching Terminal Velocity: As the tiny droplets continue to grow within the cloud, they eventually reach a point where their weight overcomes the air resistance acting against them. This is called their terminal velocity, the speed at which they fall steadily through the air. Due to their small size and mass, drizzle droplets have a very low terminal velocity, resulting in their slow and misty descent.

5. Precipitation or Evaporation: Once the droplets reach their terminal velocity, they begin to fall as drizzle. However, depending on atmospheric conditions, some drizzle droplets might evaporate before reaching the ground, especially in drier environments or when falling through a layer of warm air.

Ice crystals

Ice crystal formation is a fascinating process that transforms water vapor into the beautiful snowflakes we admire and the icy giants that fall as hail.

The Need for Change: From Vapor to Solid

  • It all starts with water vapor in the atmosphere. Unlike liquid water, water vapor molecules have high kinetic energy and move freely.
  • For ice crystals to form, the temperature needs to be below freezing (0째C or 32째F). This lowers the energy of the water vapor molecules, making them more susceptible to change.

    • Nucleation: The Seed of Change

    A critical step is nucleation. This is the formation of a tiny ice crystal seed, around which additional water molecules can gather and solidify. There are two main pathways for nucleation:
    • Homogeneous nucleation: This occurs spontaneously when a cluster of water vapor molecules by chance arrange themselves in a structure similar to ice and release energy. However, this is a rare event in the atmosphere due to the high energy required.
    • Heterogeneous nucleation: This is the more common pathway. Here, water vapor condenses on a tiny impurity like dust particles or microscopic salt crystals already present in the air. These impurities act as a "seed" with a structure that facilitates the orderly arrangement of water molecules into an ice crystal lattice.

    Growth and Shaping:Building the Crystals

    Once an ice crystal seed forms, the fun begins! As the temperature remains below freezing, water vapor molecules from the surrounding air are attracted to the seed and stick to its surface. Due to the unique arrangement of hydrogen and oxygen atoms in water molecules, they form strong hydrogen bonds with each other. This preferential bonding creates a hexagonal crystal lattice, the building block of all ice crystals. The growth of the ice crystal isn't uniform. Different crystal faces (flat surfaces) attract water molecules at slightly different rates depending on the temperature and humidity. This differential growth leads to the development of the distinctive shapes we see in snowflakes, such as hexagonal plates, columns, or feathery dendrites.
